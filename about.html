My story

I became a nutritionist because the internet almost ruined me.

At 24, after a decade of being the "healthy friend," I was bloated, anxious, exhausted, and completely convinced my body had betrayed me. I had tried every protocol Google sold — paleo, keto, intermittent fasting, raw vegan, carnivore. I had jars of supplements I couldn't pronounce. Nothing worked. Most made it worse.

What finally helped wasn't a new diet. It was sitting down with a clinical nutritionist who asked about my grandmother's cooking. She didn't hand me a plan. She helped me find my way back to dal, rice, and real cooking — backed by labs, not by wellness trends.

I went back to school for my M.Sc. in Clinical Nutrition the year after. Eight years and 500+ clients later, I'm still doing the same thing she did for me — helping women find a way of eating that feels both rigorous and tender.

What I believe

Six things I won't budge on.

Indian food is not the enemy

Rice, roti, ghee, dal, sugar — used well — are part of the answer, not the problem.

Weight is not a measure of worth

It's one of many data points. Energy, digestion, sleep, mood, cycles matter at least as much.

Labs tell the truth

Before we guess, we measure. Blood work, hormones, micronutrients — we use data, not hunches.

No meal plan is meant to last forever

A plan is a training wheel. The real work is learning to listen to your body without one.

Supplements are last resort

Food first, always. Supplements are triage for real deficiencies — not a daily routine to sell you.

Shame has no place in a consult

You ate takeout every night this week? Good, tell me honestly. I can only help with the truth.
